I’ve taught AI to 400,000+ students.

And the number one thing that separates the people who get results from Claude versus the people who say “AI is useless” is embarrassingly simple.

Their prompts.

Not the model. Not the subscription tier. Not the tool.

The prompt.

So I went through 50 creator-focused Claude prompts — covering productivity, monetisation, content, branding, client work — and sorted the ones worth your time from the ones that sound good but don’t hold up.

Here’s the honest breakdown.

---

## The Uncomfortable Truth First

Most people treat Claude like Google.

They type a vague question. They get a vague answer. They close the tab and say it’s not useful.

That’s not a Claude problem. That’s a prompting problem.

**Specific input = specific output.**

The prompts below work because they give Claude context, constraints, and a clear format to follow. That’s it. That’s the whole secret.

Now let’s get into it.

---

## ⚡ Productivity Prompts Worth Using

**The one I use the most:**

> *“Build me a content batching system so I can plan, create, edit, and schedule a full month of posts in just ONE day. Make it step-by-step.”*

I’ve run this exact prompt. It spits out a full batching workflow — what to do hour by hour, which tasks to group, what to prep the night before. Most creators wing this. You don’t have to.

**The decision filter — this one is underrated:**

> *“Build me a simple YES or NO filter so I can decide fast if a new opportunity is worth my time — without overcommitting.”*

As someone who gets pitched courses, collabs, and “exciting opportunities” weekly — having a clear framework to say no fast is one of the highest-leverage things you can build. Let Claude build it for you in 60 seconds.

**Time-blocking done right:**

> *“Create a time-blocked daily plan for me. I need deep creative work in the morning and admin tasks in the afternoon. Include buffer time and energy breaks.”*

Replace the brackets with your actual schedule constraints and energy patterns. The output is surprisingly personal.

---

## 📚 Upskilling Prompts That Actually Teach

This one is built for people who learn by doing:

> *“Design a project-based 2-week learning plan for \[AI TOOL / SKILL\]. By the end, I should have something real and working to show people.”*

I’ve used a version of this with students learning Midjourney, RunwayML, and Claude itself. The “real and working” constraint forces the output to be practical — not just a reading list.

**For honest self-assessment:**

> *“I am a \[YOUR ROLE\]. Audit my skill set. Find my gaps and blind spots. Tell me exactly what to learn next to increase my income this year.”*

The trick here is to give Claude your full context. Tell it what you’ve built, what you sell, what your revenue looks like. The more honest you are, the more uncomfortable (and useful) the output gets.

---

## 🤝 Client-Getting Prompts That Don’t Sound Robotic

Most AI-written outreach sounds exactly like AI-written outreach.

This one fights that:

> *“Write 3 cold DMs to pitch \[YOUR SERVICE\] to potential clients on Facebook or Instagram. Make each one feel personal — not copy-paste.”*

The key is adding a fourth message after: *“Now make each one shorter and more casual. Like I’m texting a friend.”*

That follow-up is what makes it usable.

**The conversion signal prompt:**

> *“List 5 signs that tell me a follower is ready to buy. For each sign, give me the exact reply to send.”*

This one surprised me. Claude’s answers here are more nuanced than most sales training I’ve seen. Worth running just to read through it.

---

## 📣 Marketing Prompts That Move People

**My favourite in this category:**

> *“List the 10 most common reasons people do NOT buy \[PRODUCT\]. For each one, write a short, persuasive reply that removes the doubt.”*

Run this before any launch. Print it out. Know every objection cold.

**The email sequence builder:**

> *“Write a 5-email welcome sequence for new subscribers interested in \[TOPIC\]. Each email should build trust and end with a soft offer.”*

Tell Claude your tone, your audience’s level of sophistication, and whether you’re selling a $9 product or a $999 one. That context shapes everything.

---

## 🏷️ Branding Prompts Worth Your Time

This format prompt does most of the heavy lifting:

> *“Write 5 brand positioning statements using this format: ‘I help \[AUDIENCE\] achieve \[RESULT\] without \[COMMON PROBLEM\].’ Make each version feel different and strong.”*

Then pick the one that makes you want to print it on a t-shirt. That’s your positioning.

**For content pillar planning:**

> *“Generate 8 content pillars for my personal brand in \[NICHE\]. Under each pillar, suggest 3 specific post ideas with a ready-to-use hook.”*

That’s 24 post ideas with hooks in one prompt. If you’re staring at a blank content calendar, start here.

---

## 💰 Monetisation Prompts I’d Run Today

**The productisation prompt:**

> *“Take this skill and turn it into 3 digital products at $9, $49, and $199 price points. Tell me what each product includes and who it is for: \[YOUR SKILL\].”*

This forces you to think in tiers instead of all-or-nothing. Most creators skip the $9 and $49 entry points and wonder why no one buys their $199 offer.

**Offer stack builder:**

> *“Build a full offer stack for me as a service creator selling \[SERVICE\] to \[AUDIENCE\]. Include pricing tiers, deliverables, and upsells.”*

Pair this with the free-to-paid funnel prompt below:

> *“Design a free-to-paid funnel that uses Facebook content to attract followers and converts them into buyers of \[PRODUCT\]. Make it simple and realistic for a solo creator.”*

Together, they give you a business model you can actually run alone.

---

## 🎬 Content Creation Prompts That Save Real Hours

**The content audit prompt — run this before you create anything:**

> *“Before I create this content, audit the idea for me. Rate the hook, relevance, shareability, and viral potential each on a scale of 1 to 10: \[PASTE IDEA\].”*

I’ve started doing this before every post. It’s like having an editor on call who gives you a score with reasoning. If the hook scores below a 7, I rewrite it.

**The contrarian content prompt:**

> *“Create 10 ‘What Most People Get Wrong About \[TOPIC\]’ content ideas for \[NICHE\]. Make them contrarian, debate-worthy, and impossible to ignore.”*

Contrarian content consistently outperforms advice content. This prompt generates the angles most creators are too cautious to touch.

**And for repurposing:**

> *“Take this long-form idea and break it into 5 short-form scripts under 60 seconds each. Give each one a punchy opening hook: \[PASTE IDEA\].”*

One idea. Five pieces of content. No extra thinking.

---

## How to Actually Use These

Don’t collect them. Use them.

Pick one category. Pick one prompt. Replace every bracket with your real, specific information. Read the output. Then type:

*“Make it more specific for \[YOUR NICHE\]”*

That follow-up is where the good stuff lives.

Claude has no memory of what you’ve told it before. Every prompt is a fresh start. So the more context you give upfront — your niche, your audience, your price point, your goals — the sharper the output.

Treat it like a briefing, not a Google search.
